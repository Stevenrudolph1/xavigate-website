Structure: Finding What's Missing

When people carry too much, the instinct is to help them carry it better. The real question is: why are they carrying it at all?

Someone is doing the work of three people. Staying late. Holding things together. Everyone calls them "dedicated." Nobody asks why the system needs a hero to function.

Usually, something is missing — a decision that was never made, a role that was never defined, a process that doesn't exist, authority that was never granted, knowledge that lives in one person's head. The person isn't the problem. The absence is. But the person is the one feeling it.

This course teaches you to find the absence, name it, and design the fix. Not a better coping strategy. Not a pep talk about delegation. An actual structural change that means the person doesn't have to carry it anymore.

Curriculum

Module 1 — The Five Absences

Organizations don't break loudly. They break by omission — something was never built, never decided, never defined. People fill the gap with personal effort, and everyone calls it leadership. This module teaches you to see the five structural absences that create heroic load.

  • Decision absence — when nobody knows who decides what
  • Role absence — when responsibilities exist without clear ownership
  • Process absence — when every situation is handled ad hoc
  • Authority absence — when people have responsibility without the power to act
  • Knowledge absence — when critical information lives in one person
  • Exercises: Identifying which absence is driving the load in real situations
Module 2 — The Audit and the Fix

Now that you can see what's missing, learn to map the full load — who is carrying what, and what the system should be holding instead. Then design interventions that actually change the structure, not just the person's response to it.

  • Load auditing — mapping what a person carries vs. what the system should handle
  • Bottleneck analysis — why one person becomes the chokepoint and what to do about it
  • Designing structural fixes that hold without someone personally maintaining them
  • When to present a structural read vs. when to let the person discover it
  • Case submissions: Full structural analysis and intervention design
