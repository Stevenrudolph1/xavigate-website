Foundations in Renergence

Learn to see where the problem is actually coming from — and stop guessing.

Who This Is For

Coaches, managers, HR professionals, consultants, therapists — anyone whose job involves figuring out why someone isn't working well and doing something about it. If you've ever watched a capable person struggle and thought "something isn't right here but I can't put my finger on it" — this is the training that gives you the finger to put on it.

The Problem You Already Know

Someone is struggling. Maybe they're visibly burned out. Maybe they're performing fine but something's off. Maybe nothing is wrong, exactly—it's just flat.

Everyone has a theory about what's wrong with them. Usually, everyone is wrong.

The real issue is almost never the person. It's a condition in the situation — something missing in how things are set up, a mismatch between the work and who they are, or a pattern in how someone is showing up. But the default move, every time, is to skip the situation and explain the person.

This course teaches you to stop doing that.

What You Walk Away With

By the end of this course, you can look at a situation where someone is struggling — obviously, invisibly, or somewhere in between — and tell whether the real issue is:

How the work is set up

Something is missing in the structure — unclear roles, heroic load, nobody carrying what needs carrying. The person looks like the problem, but the arrangement is.

How well it fits the person

The work demands one kind of engagement; the person supplies another. Sometimes the mismatch is obvious. Sometimes it hides behind competence for years.

How someone is showing up

A pattern in how someone attends — reflexive helping, chronic withdrawal, constant analysis. Not a personality flaw. A position that got stuck.

Getting this right changes everything that comes after — the conversation, the intervention, whether anything actually shifts.

How It Works

This isn't a course where you watch videos and take a quiz. It's built around doing the analysis — on real situations, from the beginning.

You read. Then you use it.

The course manual teaches the concepts with worked examples. Then exercises ask you to apply them — not to hypothetical scenarios, but to situations you actually recognize. Three modules take you from catching the default misread, to telling similar-looking problems apart, to holding your analysis under pressure.

You build a Learning Portfolio.

A set of documents (Google Docs or Word) where you work through exercises, develop case analyses, and build a body of practiced thinking. By the end, you haven't just studied something. You've built evidence of how you see.

You submit it. Steven reviews it.

Not a grade. Steven Rudolph reads your work directly in your documents — inline comments on where your analysis landed, where it drifted, and what a sharper read would look like. Plus a final review report. It's a detailed, honest read of how you're seeing.

The Three Modules

Module 1 — The Misread

Why the default explanation is almost always wrong. You learn the core premise — that when someone is burned out, quietly off, or just flat, the cause is usually a condition in the situation, not a deficit in the person — and start catching the moment where your own read goes sideways.

  • Why "the person is the problem" feels so convincing — and when it's wrong
  • The three areas where problems actually live
  • How to catch yourself mid-read when you're defaulting to the person
  • Exercises: Spotting the misread in real situations
Module 2 — Telling Them Apart

The hard part isn't knowing the three areas exist. It's telling which one is actually driving the situation when they all look similar on the surface. This module teaches the distinctions that separate a structural problem from a fit problem from a positioning problem.

  • What each area looks like in practice — and how they mimic each other
  • The evidence that tells you which area is actually driving the situation
  • Why "it could be anything" is a cop-out — and how to commit to a read
  • Exercises: Analysis on cases that could go either way
Module 3 — Holding the Read

You've made a read. Now what? This module covers what happens after — how to hold your analysis under pressure, how to question it without abandoning it, and how to present what you're seeing in a way that lands.

  • How to present a structural read without triggering defensiveness
  • When to question your own analysis — and when to trust it
  • The difference between explaining what you see and proving you're right
  • Case submissions: Full analysis on real situations
