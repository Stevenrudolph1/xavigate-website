Positioning: How You Show Up

The session went sideways. Not because of the client. Because of you — and you didn't see it until afterward.

Every practitioner has a default mode. The way you show up when you're not thinking about how you're showing up. For some, it's advice-giving. For others, it's holding space. For others, it's asking questions. None of these are wrong. But when you can't get out of one, everything looks like a nail.

The problem isn't the mode. It's getting stuck in it. When a coach only coaches, a therapist only holds space, or a consultant only advises — they're not choosing. They're defaulting. And the client pays for it, because the response they're getting isn't matched to what the situation needs.

This course teaches you to see your own patterns in real time. To catch when you've collapsed into one mode, to move deliberately when the situation calls for something else, and to recover when the way you showed up broke trust.

Curriculum

Module 1 — Your Default Mode

You have a way of showing up that feels natural. It's what you do when you're not deliberately choosing how to engage. This module helps you see that default clearly — what it looks like, when it serves you, and when it costs your clients.

  • The different ways practitioners show up — and why each one exists
  • Finding your own default: what you do when you're on autopilot
  • When your default serves the situation — and when it serves your comfort
  • What it looks like from the other side when a practitioner is stuck
  • Exercises: Identifying your default mode and its signatures
Module 2 — Deliberate Movement and Recovery

Seeing your default isn't enough. You need to move — deliberately, mid-session — when the situation calls for something different. And when you've already broken trust by showing up wrong, you need to know how to recover without making it worse.

  • Making deliberate shifts: how to move from one mode to another mid-conversation
  • The three trust violations — and what recovery looks like for each
  • When to name what happened vs. when to just shift
  • Watching yourself in real time: building the self-observation muscle
  • Case submissions: Full positioning analysis including the real-time read
